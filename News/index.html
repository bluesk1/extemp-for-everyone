    Read the News!

    My Journey to Extemp

    I wanted to give a bit of a personal story about why I do extemp (which relates to reading the news by the way). This event is near and dear to my heart. Since early 2020, I was an avid news reader. I remember days in middle school where I'd spend whole class periods scrolling through Apple News to read and garner stories from all corners of the world. During that time, COVID-19 was also emerging as an outbreak and that was an inflection point in reading the news. It seemed natural to me. At one point, I watched the press briefings from the White House every single day during March 2020. With the presidential election and many more world events, the activity of reading the news was something that intrigued me.

    Then it was my freshman year in high school, and I joined Speech and Debate. When I first started Speech and Debate, I began with doing original oratory. I very soon realized that OO wasn't the event for me. In fact, right there was lying an event that seemed practically made for me. As a passionate science enthusiast and news-reader, the challenge of extemp and its connection with current events seemed perfect for me. And that was the beginning that started an unforgettable journey in this activity.

    So why am I telling you all this?

    Well, for starters, find your connection to extemp. For me, it was the news and the science-esque nature of it. For you, it could be something else. Find the reason you love extemp, it's the thing that will drive the fun in this activity.

    Second, it's the importance of reading the news. After this long-winded personal narrative/introduction here, I've got some tips on how to actually read the news.

    Where should I read the news? And what?

    In my opinion, the best sites for reading the News are Apple News and Google News. These sites aggregate articles from a variety of source (and, therefore, viewpoints as well). In these sites, remember to look at the headlines and scroll long beyond that too. If you find an article that sounds somewhat interesting, go through it. The important thing about journalism is that it's usually meant for other people to read, so it is meant to be engaging. If you read through the news often (all parts), then the more you will be on top of things like the signing of certain legislation and its importance (these things take one extra scroll).

    Reading Articles

    There are three essential articles in the news that make up the core of it (in order of importance/frequency of reading):

    • Matter-of-fact Articles:
      These articles are very short (like ones from Reuters or Associated Press—the ones that make the headlines). They are usually to the point and summarize a presidential action, event, or a piece of news that just took place. Fun fact is that Reuters/Associated Press are the outlets that give the news to all other outlets (the main branch of news comes from them). You should read these VERY often It really helps to know what's going on. If you don't read this, it's like going to a test knowing how to do a problem, but without knowing the concepts behind it (which can be a struggle in most cases 😂).
    • Analytical Pieces These pieces are classic extemp points. Usually outlets like the Atlantic, Vox, or the Washington Post tend to have the best analytical pieces. They critically analyze an event and come to conclusions. These articles are most relevant for analysis I would suggest reading through these critically, thinking about new perspectives to challenge the author, and how it could be applied to various events. These articles are super important for the sum and substance of speeches.
    • Specific Stories I'd say these types of articles are excellent pieces of journalism that show how exactly a certain piece of news affects people. This is especially important for you, as an informed citizen, to read but also understand how what you are discussing is affecting the real-world.

    Deconstructing news-reading like this seems so weird to me. NEVER think of reading the news like a class assignment It's not homework. When you become an adult, you need to do this anyways. But even more so, think of it as engaging with the world through your lens.

    If you REALLY enjoy reading the news, at one point, I would suggest converting speeches into well-thought-out analytical pieces as we discussed above. Not only does this improve your analysis abilities in round, but it also forces you to critically engage with current events.

    Final word

    A major reason why I truly enjoyed extemp is because of the news, and I hope you can share this same enthusiasm for the activity (and hopefully for reading the news too). There are some times when we read the news and think to ourselves: wow, we really can't fix that can we? And often go into a bit of a grim mood. But I think this activity is special because of this. No, we might not be able to go above and beyond and actually fully fix the problem. But we do have a unique opportunity to offer a solution—a creative one—through our voice, and that's what makes extemp so special.
